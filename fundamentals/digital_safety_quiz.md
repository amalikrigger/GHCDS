## Answer Key (Teacher Copy)

1. **C** — Long, unique, and unpredictable. Length is the most important factor — a 16-character passphrase takes centuries to crack.

2. **B** — Delete it. The domain `amaz0n-alerts.com` (with a zero) is fake, and the urgency is a classic phishing tactic. If concerned, go to amazon.com directly (don't click the email link).

3. **B** — Even if someone steals your password, they can't log in without the second factor (your phone, authenticator app, etc.).

4. **B** — AI can write perfect grammar, personalize emails using your social media info, and mimic real company language, making them much harder to spot than old phishing emails full of typos.

5. Example: `sunshine-penguin-laptop-thunder` — This is better because it's 32 characters long (takes centuries to crack), it's random (the words aren't related to you personally), and it's easy to remember as a mental image. `Basketball2024!` is weak because it's based on common words and predictable patterns that automated tools try first.

6. Steps: (1) Don't click the link. (2) Contact your friend through a different method (text, call, or in person) and ask if they sent it. (3) If they didn't, their account may be hacked — tell them to change their password. (4) If they did, ask them to describe what the link is before you click. The key is verifying through a separate channel.
