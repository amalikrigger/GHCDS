# 🔍 Research & Learning with AI

### How to find real answers, spot junk, and use AI as a study tool — not a crutch

> **Grades:** 7th–12th &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Time:** 1-2 class periods &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Difficulty:** Beginner
>
> The internet has all the answers — and also all the wrong answers. AI can help you learn faster than ever — or make you think you know something when you don't. This lesson teaches you how to research effectively, evaluate sources, and use AI tools to actually LEARN, not just copy.

## Part 1: Searching Like a Pro

### Most People Search Wrong

When most people need information, they type a full sentence into Google:

❌ `What is the best programming language to learn for getting a job in 2026?`

This works, but it's not efficient. Google ignores most of those words. Here's how to search better:

**Use keywords, not sentences:**
✅ `best programming language jobs 2026`

**Use quotes for exact phrases:**
✅ `"entry level" software developer salary 2026`

**Use the minus sign to exclude results:**
✅ `python tutorial -youtube` (shows results without YouTube)

**Use `site:` to search within a specific website:**
✅ `site:reddit.com best laptop for students 2026`
✅ `site:stackoverflow.com python read csv file`

**Use `filetype:` to find specific file types:**
✅ `cybersecurity basics filetype:pdf`

## Part 2: Evaluating Sources — Is This Legit?

### The SIFT Method

Use this 4-step method before trusting ANY information:

**S — Stop.** Don't immediately believe or share what you read. Pause.

**I — Investigate the source.** Who wrote this? What is this website? Are they an expert or trying to sell you something?

**F — Find better coverage.** Search for the same claim in other sources. If only one site says it, be skeptical. If multiple credible sources agree, it's more likely true.

**T — Trace claims to the original.** Articles often reference studies or reports. Find the original source — the summary might be wrong or misleading.

---

### Red Flags for Unreliable Sources

| Red Flag | Example |
|---|---|
| No author name | "Staff Writer" or no attribution at all |
| No date | You don't know if it's from 2024 or 2014 |
| Emotional headlines | "SHOCKING: Scientists Can't Believe This!" |
| Ads everywhere | More ads than content = low-quality site |
| No sources cited | Makes claims without linking to evidence |
| Domain looks weird | `.biz`, `.info`, or misspelled domains |
| Only one site says it | No other credible source confirms it |

### Reliable Source Hierarchy

| Tier | Source Type | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Best** | Primary sources, peer-reviewed research | Published studies, government data (.gov), official documentation |
| **Good** | Established news/education | BBC, AP News, Reuters, university websites (.edu), MDN Web Docs |
| **Okay** | Community-vetted content | Stack Overflow (high-vote answers), Wikipedia (as a starting point, then check its sources) |
| **Use with caution** | Individual opinions | Blog posts, YouTube videos, social media (might be great, might be wrong) |
| **Avoid** | Content farms, AI-generated junk | Sites with no author, SEO-optimized filler, "Top 10" listicles from unknown sites |

## Part 3: Using AI to Learn (Not to Cheat)

### The Right Way vs. The Wrong Way

**Wrong way (cheating):** "Write my essay about the American Revolution."
You learn nothing. You submit someone else's work. You can't answer questions about it.

**Right way (learning):** "I'm writing an essay about the American Revolution. Can you explain the main causes in simple terms so I understand them before I write?"
You learn the material. You write in your own words. You can discuss it in class.

Here's the key difference: **Are you using AI to understand, or to avoid understanding?**

---

### AI as a Study Tool — Powerful Prompts

Here's how to use AI tools to actually learn:

**Explain a concept:**
```
Explain how CSS Flexbox works like I'm in 8th grade. Use a real example.
```

**Quiz yourself:**
```
Give me 5 practice questions about Python loops. Don't show the answers until I try each one.
```

**Debug understanding:**
```
I think a for loop in Python always runs exactly 10 times. Am I right? If not, explain why.
```

**Summarize and simplify:**
```
Summarize this article in 3 bullet points a middle schooler would understand: [paste article]
```

**Get different perspectives:**
```
What are the arguments for AND against using AI in schools? Give me both sides fairly.
```

**Break down a process:**
```
Walk me through how to set up a Raspberry Pi web server step by step. Explain why each step matters, not just what to do.
```

### AI Pitfalls — What Can Go Wrong

**1. Hallucinations** — AI makes stuff up. It generates text that *sounds* right but is completely false. It will confidently cite studies that don't exist, quote people who never said that, and state false facts.

**How to protect yourself:** Always verify important facts from AI with a real source. Especially statistics, quotes, dates, and scientific claims.

**2. Overreliance** — If you use AI to answer every question without thinking first, you stop building your own knowledge. It's like using a calculator for 2+2 — eventually you can't do basic math without it.

**How to protect yourself:** Try to answer questions yourself FIRST. Then use AI to check, expand, or clarify your understanding.

**3. Outdated information** — AI models have knowledge cutoff dates. They may not know about recent events, new software versions, or current prices.

**How to protect yourself:** For current information, use web search or AI tools with web access (like Claude with search, or Perplexity).

**4. Bias** — AI reflects the biases in its training data. It may present one perspective as universal truth.

**How to protect yourself:** Ask AI for multiple perspectives. Compare AI responses with diverse sources.

## Part 4: Putting It All Together

### The Research Workflow

When you need to learn about something new, follow this process:

1. **Start with a quick search** — get an overview of the topic
2. **Use AI to explain confusing parts** — ask it to simplify or give examples
3. **Verify key facts** — check AI's claims against reliable sources
4. **Go deeper with primary sources** — read the original documentation, study, or article
5. **Test your understanding** — can you explain it to someone else without looking at notes?

> **The test:** If you can explain something in your own words to a friend and answer their follow-up questions, you actually understand it. If you can only repeat what AI told you, you don't.

## 🧠 Vocabulary to Know

| Term | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **SIFT Method** | Stop, Investigate, Find better coverage, Trace to original — a way to evaluate sources |
| **Hallucination** | When AI generates false information that sounds confident and correct |
| **Primary Source** | The original source of information (a study, official data, eyewitness account) |
| **Secondary Source** | Someone else's interpretation of a primary source (news article, blog post) |
| **Knowledge Cutoff** | The date an AI's training data ends — it doesn't know what happened after |
| **Prompt** | The text you type into an AI tool to get a response |
| **SEO** | Search Engine Optimization — techniques to rank higher in Google (not always = quality) |
| **Content Farm** | A website that produces low-quality articles just to get ad clicks |
